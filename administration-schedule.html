Time and Schedules

One of the more complex challenges associated with digital, is managing the many competing and shifting priorities. A “course schedule” or “course calendar” is a staple element of any course. Whether f2f, remote or blended.

If class are only face to face, a calendar allows students to see when they need to arrive at the venue (lecture hall, classroom or lab) or  and how long the schedule session will take.

Off campus students do not have the luxury of dedicated study time and are required to juggle conflicting priorities (school, family, children, work). Students, because of their location, have to make more decisions about attention. Although studying remotely might have the benefit of making studying more flexible, students own time management requires explicit support, especially if they are unfamiliar with being a self-directed student, struggle.

Scheduling becomes an acute issue in a hybrid course where online activities are required to be completed between the face-to-face classes. In the hybrid setting, especially when learning behind a screen and in face to face classes, there is a greater need to make the sequence and completion of  course-related events apparent to all members of a course. This includes lecturer availability; due dates for activities, such as quizzes, assignment dates, dates and times for face-to-face sessions; and (if you are going to meet synchronously) online meeting times.

Below are a few tips on scheduling and communicating clear, time specific teaching and learning events, so that all students, whether on campus, remote or shifting in between can plan and manage their time, in advance.

Tips for scheduling

  • Be explicit about the time commitment for all course related events.
  • If the course event occurs online, remember there may be time zones that may affect the time for the online and/or in-person event.
  • Create a course map that illustrates a timeline of online and offline content, interaction, and assessment elements required each week in your course.
  • Look at your LMS and check to see what items with due dates do (or do not) appear in the LMS calendar.
  • Describe the relationship between online activities and in class activities.
  • Use iCal files (the standard internet calendar format) to create and share electronic calendars.

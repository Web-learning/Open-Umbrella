To Consider: E-assessment Automation

Multiple forms of assessment are possible with e-assessment. Question types such as multiple choice, true or false, matching responses and so forth, can be used with a bank of questions to test a large class easily and release marks automatically. For lecturers, especially those with high enrolment numbers, a reduced marking load is welcome.

Types of questions that are suitable for aoto marking

Types of questions that can be used for auto marking

  • Multiple choice questions -the student has to choose one answer from a list' of possible answers
  • Multiple response questions the student has to select more than one answer from a list of possible answers
  • Alternative response question - the student has to choose between just two items. Students have a 50% chance of guessing the correct answer
  • Ranking questions - the student re-arranges a list of items in a column. Used to test the knowledge of sequences, order of events, level of gradation
  • Matching question - the student sees a set of statements and a set of responses. They have to decide which response from the second list corresponds or matches each statement in the first list
  • Completion question - the student is given a statement and key words are omitted. Students have to complete the statement by filling in the word(s).
  • Assertion/reason question – Students have to decide whether the assertion, statement and explanation are true, and if true, whether the explanation is a valid reason for the statement. The questions combines elements of multiple choice and true/false question types.

The pros and cons of computer assisted assessment

The benefits of auto e-assessment need to balanced against the draw backs. Careful thought needs to be applied when using to use automated forms of assessment .

Immediacy – If students see their test results immediately, their interest is piqued, and they can respond and take action

Impersonality – Although students are getting feedback on their performance, the human touch is missing. Students may become excessively discouraged if they encounter frequent poor results

Location and time independent – Students can take a test from anywhere and at any time

Cheating – It is easier to impersonate another student or bring in additional aids to the testing situation

Automatic score recording – results can be logged automatically, and optionally made immediately visible for student access

Technology Problems – Technical problems (e.g. failed hardware, phone line outages etc.) may need to be allowed for, and participants should be encouraged to document these problems rather than just complain, that “it doesn't work."

Timeliness – if the e-assessment is used as a learning check, then the timing can be set up so the e-assessment is available at a specific time or date

Time Commitment – Although e-assessments can decrease your overall workload, building the e-assessments take time

Improved access – On and off campus students can do the e-assessment

Students require some technology skills – Students need to have at least baseline competencies in using the technologies


10 Common errors

Common pitfalls to avoid when setting short questions for automarking

  1. The position of the correct answer (e.g., A, B, C, D) does not vary
  2. Using double negatives or absolutes such as “never” or “always”
  3. Using NOT or EXCEPT in the question
  4. Implausible or obviously wrong answers (distractors).
  5. Distractors provide unintended cues to the correct answer
  6. Distractors overlap
  7. Distractors provide unintended cues to the correct answer
  8. Grammatical clues offer a hint to the correct answer (e.g., the use of “a” or “an”).
  9. Including “all of the above”, as students only need to know that two of the options are correct to answer
  10. Including “none of the above” as this tests ability to detect incorrect answers, but not whether they know the correct answer.
